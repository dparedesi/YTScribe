summary: This presentation explores AWS Outposts as a solution for bringing cloud infrastructure to on-premises edge locations, with a detailed case study from Rivian's manufacturing facility journey. Matt Reed introduces AWS Outposts as a fully managed service that delivers AWS infrastructure and services to on-premises locations, available in full 42U rack form factors for data centers and smaller 1U/2U servers for regional offices or smaller facilities. The Generation 2 Outpost rack features decoupled networking architecture that eliminates the 1-to-1 compute requirement, increases cost efficiency through flexible deployment, enables incremental capacity additions without full rack purchases, improves networking resilience with inherent device failover capabilities, and simplifies connectivity through domain routing groups and customer-owned IP (COIP) setups for services like RDS requiring multi-AZ failover. Rivian's Vinny Maccachek shares their three-phase journey: initially attempting to migrate legacy VM-hosted manufacturing workloads by snapshotting VMs and moving them to Outposts, which failed due to network resilience issues, lack of application recovery strategy, and failure to standardize on containers despite having internal Kubernetes expertise. The second phase involved building their own Manufacturing Container Platform (MCP) using disparate vendor tools, which led to ticket hell with multiple vendors, capacity planning challenges requiring 6-12 month lead times, and lack of standardization preventing workload portability between cloud EKS and on-premises Kubernetes due to missing features like IAM roles for service accounts. The third and current phase involves going all-in on Outpost Generation 2 with proper architecture including Direct Connect for resilient service link connectivity instead of public internet, EKS for feature parity with cloud workloads enabling their Kubernetes platform team to advise on migrations, and proper resilience planning. Key lessons learned include the critical importance of architecting for network resilience since Outposts require connectivity back to AWS regions for orchestration services, the need for capacity management dashboards and lead time planning despite reduced procurement cycles compared to traditional vendors, the recommendation to share VPCs rather than creating hundreds of separate VPCs to reduce operational overhead given finite power, cooling, and CIDR constraints, and the recognition that trust is hard-earned, easily lost, and difficult to reestablish with internal customers after failures. The outcomes include consolidation onto a single platform, improved portability between cloud and edge, low latency for manufacturing processes, and cognitive simplification by using AWS consistently across the organization rather than maintaining separate skill silos and legacy infrastructure.
