	The session presents "Rapid Review and Assessment," an open-source, AI-enabled workflow for transforming regulatory document review into a scalable, auditable process that accelerates sustainability compliance rather than creating administrative drag. Using a concrete building-permit example, the talk explains the two persistent challenges of applying off-the-shelf generative AI to regulation: context dilution when a single model ingests hundreds of pages and checklist items, and an accountability gap that leaves regulators unable to rely solely on AI judgments. Rapid’s design tackles both by splitting review into two tightly controlled phases: structured checklist extraction followed by itemized AI review with human validation. Phase one uses generative models to parse a PDF checklist into structured items; an expert then verifies and clarifies ambiguous entries so the knowledge base reflects exact compliance criteria. Phase two maps each checklist item to the relevant documents and runs a focused AI assessment on each item individually, returning a pass/fail determination with a confidence score, transparent reasoning, and source citations. Low-confidence cases or disputed results are routed to humans for adjudication, preserving human oversight and an auditable trail. Architecturally the project runs serverless on AWS: a static front end in S3/CloudFront with WAF, API Gateway and Cognito for authentication, Step Functions coordinating Lambda-based workflows, and Amazon Bedrock agents for model execution and external knowledge access; results persist in Amazon RDS for relational queries. The Bedrock agent core can call tools or knowledge bases (including custom computational checks) so the system can compute standards-based requirements rather than rely on vague prompts. Because the AI operates on a single checklist item with tightly scoped context, the model’s accuracy improves and error modes are easier to detect and correct. Serverless economics means customers can pilot with a handful of weekly documents and scale to tens of thousands per week without heavy infrastructure overhead, paying only for consumption. Early pilots report up to 75% reduction in human review hours, dramatically shortening permit cycles and lowering the cost of compliance while enabling regulators to retain ambitious sustainability rules.
