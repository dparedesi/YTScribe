summary: Ryan McMahon from Cambridge Mobile Telematics and Ravi from the Amazon Analytics team present their collaborative work building StreetVision, a revolutionary geospatial platform that leverages AWS Redshift spatial capabilities to predict and prevent road crashes by analyzing massive volumes of driving behavior data. Cambridge Mobile Telematics operates with a mission to make the world's roads and drivers safer, addressing the tragic reality of over 40,000 annual fatalities in the US and more than one million globally. Historically, the company focused on measuring individual driver risk by processing sensor data from feature phones, smartphones, proprietary tags, and expanding sensor platforms to identify behaviors that lead to crashes, but they recognized that crash risk extends beyond individual drivers to include vehicle and road factors. The breakthrough came when CMT shifted focus to road risk prediction, identifying where crashes will happen rather than simply reacting to past incidents, since individual crashes themselves are not predictive of future crash locations. This required processing over one trillion data points daily and aggregating behaviors like hard braking events to reveal patterns invisible to traditional analysis, such as occluded stop signs discovered through concentrated human behavior patterns rather than visual inspection. The challenge was displaying this massive geospatial information in actionable formats for departments of transportation, highway safety offices, and emergency services, leading to the decision to use H3 hexagonal hierarchical indexing rather than traditional road segments. AWS Redshift spatial capabilities became the critical enabler, as Ryan emphasizes that without AWS support for this technology developed just 18 months prior through direct partnership, the solution would not have been possible. Ravi explains that Redshift excels at spatial data processing due to its massively parallel processing architecture from day one as the first cloud-native data warehouse, columnar data storage enabling vectorized scans for efficient querying at petabyte scale, and highly precise numerically accurate spatial implementations supporting WGS84 standards and over 100 spatial functions following PostGIS syntax. The platform supports GEOMETRY and GEOGRAPHY as native first-class data types, various spatial formats including shape files, well-known text and binary, and geojson, while recently adding H3 functions that represent a game-changer for spatial lookups at scale. H3 indexing eliminates expensive Cartesian joins by using pre-computed indexes where each cell's index is the location itself across 16 resolution levels, delivering lightning-fast lookups that save 98% of compute resources and up to 85% on Redshift charges compared to traditional polygon-based queries. The platform integrates with Amazon Q SQL through Redshift Query Editor v2, enabling non-SQL experts to query the data warehouse using natural language processing with full query history, metadata, and schema context, while generative SQL capabilities predict query intent and allow notebook-style editing, creating what Ryan terms "Zillow for road safety" that translates complex risk data into actionable insights across the globe.
